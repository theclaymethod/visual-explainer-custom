The queue that outgrew itself.

How a 500 rps ceiling hid inside a healthy dashboard — and the partition key that took us to 4,200.

01 · Opening p. 02

What looked like a spike turned out to be a ceiling.

The ingest dashboard said throughput was fine. The queue depth graph said otherwise. Between the two of them, we lost three customers before anyone thought to reconcile the gap.

This is the story of what we found when we finally did.

02 · Diagnosis p. 05

The graphs disagreed. Only one of them was lying.

Ingest throughput was averaged across every worker, every tenant, every message type. It looked flat at 480 rps because a single noisy tenant couldn't move the mean.

Queue depth was per-partition. It told the real story: two partitions were saturated, thirty-six were idle, and the saturated ones belonged to customers paying us the most money.

Latency

P99 down 68%.

Head-of-line blocking from oversized payloads is gone. Small tenants stopped waiting behind the one large one.

Throughput

8.4× ceiling.

Same 24 workers, same broker cluster, same hardware budget. No horizontal scaling event. No page.

Cost

No change.

The fix was a partition key, not more boxes. Cloud bill for the month was flat within rounding error.

Regressions

Zero so far.

Per-tenant ordering was already our contract. Partitioning by tenant_id enforces what we already promised.

03 · Reframe p. 07
Reframe

Partition by tenant, not by type.

We had been partitioning on message type because the topic was called events. The name was lying too. Nothing downstream cared what kind of event it was. Everything downstream cared whose event it was.

04 · The fix

One line in the topic config.

We changed the partition key from event.type to tenant_id. The rest of the architecture stayed untouched. Same producers, same workers, same schema.

Messages now fan out across every partition instead of piling up on the two that happened to handle payload.large.

05 · Revise p. 10

What we'd do differently.

Stop naming topics after the message. Start naming them after the routing key. events-by-tenant would have raised the right question in design review six months earlier.

Alarm on per-partition saturation, not just aggregate throughput. The dashboard should have been screaming on Jan 9. It wasn't even flashing.

And: when two graphs disagree, treat it as a P1 until you know which one is wrong. Don't average.
